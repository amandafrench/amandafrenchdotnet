There is clearly no evidence of a fixed-form villanelle earlier than 1751, when Berthelin made his "augmentations" to Richelet's Dictionnaire de rimes. Despite Berthelin's audacity in having silently fixed the form of the villanelle in that year, however, no poet seems to have noticed for almost a century. Then Théodore de Banville, with the publication of "Villanelle de Buloz" (1845), became the second poet in literary history to produce a "fixed-form villanelle": he added two tercets to the length of Passerat's "J'ay perdu" but otherwise observed the strictures of its form. […] Amanda French suggests that Banville had recently encountered Passerat's "J'ay perdu" and a description of the villanelle's form in Wilhelm Ténint's volume  Prosodie de l'école moderne  (1844). Berthelin appears to be the source for Ténint, although, in pointing out the obvious fact that the last tercet before the final stanza must end with the second refrain, Ténint seems to imply that villanelles may vary in length from Passerat's model. Banville later included a definition of the fixed-form villanelle in his own prosodic treatise,  Petit traité de poésie française  (1872), which was the text responsible for popularizing the form in England by way of the poets Edmund Gosse and Austin Dobson.
